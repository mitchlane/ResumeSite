Negative Volumes

One of my tasks for OpenVSP was to implement "negative volumes" for its CFD mesh routine. The subroutine for determining if a triangle in the mesh should be deleted or not was never designed for such functionalit, so a large code refactor was needed. Along with negative volumes, I included "transparent volumes," or objects that are not affected when overlapping with another "positive volume, but should be deleted if overlapping with a "negative volume."

The number of edge cases made finding a concise solution difficult. For example, my algorithm had to know how to handle a negative volume inside a negative volume inside a transparent volume inside a positive volume. Fringe cases such as these made determining whether to delete a mesh triangle or not difficult.

Ergonomics Analysis of Touchscreens in Vehicles - Android Application

During my 2015 Summer vacation, my beautiful girlfriend requested that I create a mobile application to assist in recording data of various subjects performing a variety of tasks. She needed this data to investigate whether or not gender and hand dominance impact automobile operators' abilities to navigate a vehicle while interfacing with a touchscreen.

The upper left image is the homescreen of the application. Users select a task to complete by taping on one of the three images.

The first task is shown in the upper right corner. On each tap on a numbered box, the numbers are randomly to different boxes. When a user was asked to complete this task, they were told to select a specific number three times. When they complete the task, they hit the button labeled "Done." Then, their time is recorded and they are returned to the homescreen.

The bottom left image shows the drag task. Users were asked to drag the grey circle along the slider until the number at the end of the silder matched the number above the slider. Like the previous task, their time was recorded.

I completed this task on my own time with no prior Android experience.

3D Sphere Collision in Maya

As a final project for my parallel programming applied to computer animation class, my partner and I chose to analyze the speedup from the host to a Xeon Phi as the number of particles in a cube increase. The collision detection algorithm has a complexity of O(n^2).

As the results from the chart show, little improvement was found by offloading the the programs computing 2,000 and 10,000 particles, but quite a large speedup was found by offloading the 100,000 particle program.

The bottom picture shows all cores on the Xeon Phi being occupied while running the 100,000 particle program.
